Projection Bias and No-Show Behavior in Outpatient Allergy Scheduling

Independent research project. Outpatient allergy clinic scheduling data combined with public pollen monitoring data.

This study investigates why patients miss outpatient allergy appointments and proposes that the mechanism is behavioral, not logistical. Drawing on projection bias (Loewenstein, O'Donoghue and Rabin, 2003), the central hypothesis is that patients who book appointments during high-symptom periods systematically overestimate how much they will still need care when the appointment date arrives. When symptoms subside, the perceived urgency evaporates.

The allergy clinic is a uniquely powerful setting to test this because the patient's symptom state at booking normally unobservable can be proxied objectively using daily public pollen count data. For every appointment, I can measure the symptom-decay score: the difference in pollen conditions between booking date and appointment date. The core prediction is that no-show probability is highest when this gap is largest, controlling for lead time, time of day, day of week, season, and weather.

The research also draws on the hot-cold empathy gap: patients in visceral discomfort at the time of booking cannot accurately forecast how they will feel in a symptom-free state weeks later. If confirmed, the findings directly support low-cost operational interventions targeted reminders for high-risk bookings, seasonal lead time caps, and evidence-based overbooking models all acting on the schedule rather than the patient.

EV Charging Infrastructure and Consumer Behavior

Independent research contribution to a faculty-led project, UC San Diego Economics Department. Variable framework, R-based data pipeline, and behavioral economics analysis represent the author's independent contribution.

This research investigates why EV charging supply and demand diverge systematically across California neighborhoods. An R-based data pipeline was engineered to convert raw California DMV vehicle registration records into zip-code-level EV adoption metrics, categorizing vehicles by manufacturer, fuel type, and plug compatibility to map infrastructure gaps.

The analysis modeled the why behind geographic variation: housing density, renter-occupancy rates, income, education, and commute patterns as proxies for adoption behavior. A variable framework spanning socioeconomic, transportation, infrastructure, policy, and behavioral dimensions was connected to theory including rational inattention, price salience, and nudge theory examining how price transparency shapes consumer charging choices and firm pricing strategy under competition.
